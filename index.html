Case Study · UX Redesign · Health Tech

Good content nobody could find.

Members couldn’t get to the resources they needed. I rebuilt the platform around that problem.

Role Sole UX Designer
Deliverables UX Redesign · Health Tech · Research · End-to-End
Measurable Engagement ↑
TL;DR
Problem Members couldn’t get to the health resources. The site was broken, stale, and gave them no reason to return.
Users Wellness champion members, the Account Managers (AM), and Product Manager (PM) who supported them.
Solution Rebuilt navigation, fixed content delivery, and gave members direct access to health resources.
Impact Members could rely on the site. AM had a reason to recommend it again. PMs no longer had to coordinate material delivery.
5
Pages designed
3
Root problems solved
↑
Engagement after launch

The Challenge

Built to help.
Too broken to trust.

Members weren’t getting to the health resources because the site was hard to navigate. Links broke, and the visual design didn’t feel trustworthy enough to come back to. The site looked and functioned like a standalone landing page — no depth, no clear paths, and nowhere to go after the first scroll.

Click to enlarge

Original site · before redesign

The latest resources never made it
Even when members arrived, they couldn’t find what they needed. Content was out of date and the latest resources hadn’t made it to the site yet.
Account Managers saw no value in it
Monthly health topics were already going out by email. Without a clear reason to send members to the site, Account Managers stopped recommending it.
New content never reached members
The Product Manager was regularly uploading new health resources internally. None of it was reaching the people it was meant for.

My Role

Designer, start to launch.

My focus was making sure the experience actually worked. I mapped the flows, designed from mid-fi to hi-fi, and stayed involved through launch.

Interviewed the Product Manager and Account Managers. The AMs came with member frustrations straight from their weekly calls.
Built the site map from scratch and designed through every stage, mid-fi to hi-fi.
Worked with the brand designer to keep the visual direction true to ActiveHealth guidelines.
Ran usability testing with real members, caught one real issue, and fixed it before launch.

Research

Two insights. One clear path.

The project started as a platform migration. One conversation with the PM changed that. They was manually sending materials to AMs to share to members. That became the real opportunity. Giving members direct access to content became part of the redesign.

Fix the foundation first
Before members would engage with content, the site needed to be reliable. Consistent navigation, no broken links, downloads that worked in one click, and a layout that held up on mobile.
Freshness drives return visits
The main reason members came back was the monthly content update. If they couldn’t count on it being there and easy to find, they stopped coming back.

PM + AM stakeholder interviews · two confirmed root causes

Design Process

Map it. Wire it. Build it.

I started with a site map before touching anything visual, and a question arose. Could a member land anywhere on this site and immediately know where they were and what to do next? That meant clear navigation categories, pages that explained themselves, and an Account Manager callout on every page so help was always one step away.

01
Clear navigation categories
Members were getting lost because everything was lumped together. Four clear sections fixed that.
02
Every page explains itself
If a member landed anywhere on the site, they should know immediately where they were and what to do next.
03
Persistent Account Manager callout
Every page needed a way for members to reach their Account Manager if they had questions. I put it on every page so it was always one step away.
Click to enlarge

Mid-fi → hi-fi · 5 pages · click to explore

"

Major improvement from the original site.

Product Manager, usability testing

Testing & Iteration

The conversation fix.

Testing surfaced one issue. Members were confused by the new content that fell outside the regular monthly update. The fix was a communication strategy. Account Managers explained new sections on their weekly calls and a one-time ‘What’s New’ email introduced the change to members.

Testing revealed one gap · solved with a comm plan

Final Solution

A site members could
actually depend on.

The redesigned toolkit gave members a platform they could rely on. Clear navigation, content within reach, and a consistent call to action to reach their Account Manager on every page.

Click to enlarge

Final redesigned toolkit · clean, reliable, trusted

Members returning
Content engagement
After launch, members were finally finding what they needed. As a result, downloads, opens, and click rates increased.
More supported
Member sentiment
Members reported feeling more supported in their wellness journey. It was the first time that had ever been formally tracked.
One reliable hub
For AMs and the PM
Account Managers had one place to point members to. The Product Manager finally had visibility into how content was being used.
Trust before content.

Members weren’t disengaged because the resources were bad. They stopped coming back because of the platform. Great content means nothing if people can’t get to it easily.

Case Study · Web Design · B2B

I built a digital home from scratch.

The CVS Healthspire sales team was losing leads after every conference because there was nowhere to send people. I built them a website that changed that.

Role Design Consultant · Lead Developer
Deliverables Web Design · 0→1 Build · B2B · Design + Dev
Launched On Time
TL;DR
Problem No website meant tradeshow conversations went nowhere. Prospects had no place to go after the pitch.
Users Senior pharma and healthcare decision-makers evaluating vendors post-tradeshow.
Solution Designed and built a 3-page responsive website from scratch in 12 weeks, in time for the next conference.
Impact Sales team finally had a destination to send prospects. The brand had a credible digital home.

The Challenge

Great conversations.
No place to send people.

CVS Healthspire was having strong tradeshow conversations. Prospects were interested but when they walked away, there was nowhere to send them. No website, no way to follow up, no way to keep the conversation going.

For prospective clients
After a tradeshow conversation, there was nowhere to go. Interested prospects couldn't find Healthspire online, couldn't verify it was real, and couldn't revisit what they'd heard on their own time. The conversation just ended.
For the sales team
Every follow-up started from scratch. No URL to share, nothing to back up the pitch, and no way to keep a good conversation going.

One root cause · no digital home

My Role

Designer, advisor,
and developer.

I consulted on design, advised on technical feasibility, and built the final site. I kept the PM grounded in what was achievable and reviewed all wireframes before they went to development.

Ensured stakeholder expectations remained actionable and within technical scope.
Reviewed every Figma design and components with the designer for platform feasibility. What looks good in a mockup doesn't always survive contact with a CMS.
Built and delivered the responsive site from start to finish.

Research

Decision-makers need to scan, then trust.

Senior pharma and healthcare decision-makers, post-tradeshow. Busy people who’d already heard the pitch and weren’t looking to be sold to again.

Primary Insight
"They need to come back on their own terms, read it when it suits them, and find the contact when they’re ready."

Credibility hinges on simplicity

Design Process

Three pages. Everything it needed.

Three pages, intentional and focused, ready for launch. Healthspire was a new division still establishing itself. The site needed to make it easy to reach out, explain what they do, and build credibility.

01
A clear path to contact
The contact form gave decision-makers a direct line to the team. A conversation to find the right services for their needs.
02
Everything on one page
A decision-maker revisiting after a tradeshow shouldn’t have to dig. Everything they needed to know about Healthspire lived on the homepage.
03
Design for credibility
Healthspire was a CVS brand with no digital presence of its own. A clean layout, professional imagery, and the CVS signature heart used throughout established credibility.

Wireframes

I reviewed every design before it got built.

Some components had locked styles that couldn’t take the Healthspire brand colors in time for launch. I flagged it early so we designed around those constraints cleanly, instead of shipping something we’d have to fix later.

One round of feedback
that made it its own.

The first round leaned too hard on the CVS parent brand. Stakeholders wanted Healthspire to feel connected but distinct. Introducing the Healthspire teal in the second round gave the site its own identity without losing the CVS connection. Some components had locked styles that couldn’t take the new brand colors in time for launch. I flagged it early so we designed around those constraints cleanly rather than shipping something we’d have to fix later.

Unique brand identity within CVS guidelines

Final Solution

A professional digital
home, launched on time.

The site launched before the next conference. The sales team finally had a CVS Healthspire site to point prospects to. They had a place to follow up, learn more, and reach out to the right person.

Delivered before the next conference. The sales team had a professional site ready to share.
Without a website, you don’t exist.

In B2B healthcare, a website is proof that you’re real. The most important thing I built wasn’t a homepage. It was a reason for prospects to trust the company enough to reach out.

Case Study · Print UX · Medicare

A letter that needed to work for people.

Medicare members were missing their benefits because the letter was too hard to read. I redesigned it around them.

Role Sole UX Designer
Deliverables Print UX · Accessibility · Healthcare · Letter Redesign
Approved Program-Wide
TL;DR
Problem Medicare members were missing their benefits because the letter meant to help them was too hard to read.
Users Medicare members 65 and older managing their health benefits.
Solution Redesigned hierarchy, increased type size to meet accessibility standards, and surfaced the contact number.
Impact Approved across the entire program. Became the voice and tone benchmark for all member communications.
65+
Primary audience
2
Accessibility issues solved
1
Sole designer, start to finish

The Problem

The letter wasn't
working for its readers.

Medicare members were calling support just to find a phone number buried in their letter. The type was too small for a 65+ audience, nothing had visual priority, and the one thing they needed to act on was the hardest thing to find.

Document hierarchy redesigned

Research

I talked to the people
closest to the problem.

Interviewing the Account Representatives confirmed two real issues. At 1-2pt below accessibility standards, the type was too small for a 65+ audience. The contact number, buried in body and disclaimer copy, was nearly impossible to find.

Font size below accessibility standards
The type was 1–2pt too small for a 65+ audience, and the dense layout gave it no room to breathe. Reading it was exhausting.
Contact number impossible to find
It appeared once in body copy, then again in even smaller disclaimer text. Members couldn't locate it when they needed it most.

2 root causes confirmed

Design Response

Three changes that
solved both problems.

01
Move the contact info to the top
The decorative header image was replaced with a contact block. The phone number became the first thing members saw when they opened the letter.
02
Create a scannable sidebar
Key benefits moved into a shaded sidebar so members could read at a glance without hunting through paragraphs.
03
Rebuild the typography
Larger base size, real visual hierarchy, and enough breathing room to make the letter feel approachable rather than exhausting.

Iteration

One round of feedback,
thoughtfully addressed.

Stakeholders wanted imagery back. Photos felt warm but didn’t add meaning for someone scanning their benefits. I brought in thematic icons instead. Warmth without losing the clarity we’d built.

One ask, one clean fix · approved unanimously

Final Solution

Approved across
the whole program.

What started as a Medicare letter ended up reshaping how the whole program communicates with members.

Click to enlarge

Final letter · clear, accessible, scannable

Found immediately
Contact info

Clearer
Readability
Stakeholders called it a significant readability improvement. Designed for members 65 and older, it ended up serving a much wider audience.
Bigger impact
Full rollout
Member engagement across the program rose. A clear sign the redesign was working.
Good design is medium agnostic.

The fundamentals of a letter and an app are the same. Clear hierarchy, obvious next steps, and type that works for the reader.
