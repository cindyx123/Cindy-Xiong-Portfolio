Case Study · UX Redesign · Health Tech

Good content nobody could find or trust.

The ActiveHealth Wellness Champion Toolkit had genuinely useful content that nobody could get to. Navigation was broken, links failed, and new materials the Product Manager uploaded never reached the people they were meant for. I led the redesign from the first stakeholder interview to launch.

The Challenge

Built to help.
Too broken to trust.

The ActiveHealth Wellness Champion Toolkit was built to help members access health resources and support their wellness champions. Navigation was confusing, links broke regularly, and new content never reliably reached the people it was meant for.

Members couldn’t rely on it
Broken links, outdated content, and a slow CMS made the site feel unreliable. Members stopped returning because they couldn’t trust what they’d find.
Account Managers saw no value in it
Monthly health topics were already going out by email. Without a clear reason to send members to the site, Account Managers stopped recommending it.
New content never reached members
The Product Manager was regularly uploading new health resources internally. None of it was reaching the people it was meant for.

My Role

Designer, start to launch.

I mapped the flows, designed from the first rough wireframe through to final hi-fi, and stayed involved through launch. The brand designer handled visual polish. I focused on making sure the experience actually worked.

Interviewed the Product Manager and Account Managers — the AMs came with member frustrations straight from their weekly calls.
Built the site map from scratch and designed through every stage, lo-fi to hi-fi.
Worked with the brand designer to keep the visual direction true to ActiveHealth guidelines.
Ran usability testing with real members, caught one real issue, and fixed it before launch.

End-to-end ownership across every design phase

Research

Two insights. One clear path.

The AMs came with member frustrations straight from their weekly calls. Two things kept surfacing, and both had clear design answers.

Fix the foundation first
Before members would engage with content, the site needed to be reliable. Consistent navigation, no broken links, downloads that worked in one click, and a layout that held up on mobile.
Freshness drives return visits
The main reason members came back was the monthly content update. If they couldn’t count on it being there and easy to find, they stopped coming back.

PM + AM stakeholder interviews — two confirmed root causes

Design Process

Map it. Wire it. Build it.

I started with a site map to get the full picture before touching anything visual. From there it was lo-fi to hi-fi.

01
Restructured the navigation
Members were getting lost because everything was lumped together. Three clear categories fixed that.
02
Every page explains itself
If a member landed anywhere on the site, they should know immediately what they’d find there.
03
Persistent account manager callout
Every page needed a way for members to reach their Account Manager. I added it to the footer so it was always there, no matter where they were.

Site map → lo-fi wireframes → hi-fi

"

Downloads were quick and intuitive. Navigation was praised for clarity.

Usability testing — PM, Account Managers & pilot member group

Testing & Iteration

The fix wasn't a redesign.
It was a conversation.

Usability testing surfaced one issue: members were confused about content that wasn’t part of the regular monthly update. The fix wasn’t a design change. Account Managers started explaining new sections on their weekly calls, and we added a “What’s New” email each time content updated.

Testing revealed one gap — solved with a comm plan, not a redesign

Final Solution

A site members could
actually depend on.

The redesigned toolkit gave members immediate access to the monthly content they came for, a navigation structure that made sense, and a consistent way to reach their Account Manager.

Final redesigned toolkit — clean, reliable, trusted

More downloads
Content engagement
Both supplemental health materials and overall toolkit downloads increased after launch. Members were actually getting to the content.
More supported
Member sentiment
Members reported feeling more supported in their wellness journey. This was the first time that sentiment had been formally tracked.
One reliable hub
For AMs and the PM
Account Managers had a single place to point members to. The Product Manager, for the first time, had visibility into how content was actually being used.
Trust before content.

The content was never the problem. The toolkit had genuinely useful health resources. Members just couldn’t get to them reliably. Trust in a platform has to come before engagement with what’s on it. You can’t skip that step. Once the foundation was solid, everything else followed.

The Challenge

Great conversations.
No place to send people.

CVS Healthspire relied on tradeshows to generate leads. After each event, the sales team handed out pamphlets. Without a website, there was no way for interested prospects to follow up, verify credibility, or revisit the conversation.

For prospective clients
After a tradeshow conversation, there was nowhere to go. Interested prospects couldn't find Healthspire online, couldn't verify it was real, and couldn't revisit what they'd heard on their own time. The conversation just ended.
For the sales team
Every follow-up started from scratch. No shared destination, no credibility anchor, no way to keep the momentum from a good conversation going.

Two distinct problems, one root cause — no digital home

My Role

Designer, advisor,
and developer.

I advised on what was buildable, worked alongside the designer in Figma, and then built the final site.

Kept the PM and stakeholders grounded in what was actually buildable.
Reviewed every Figma component with the designer for platform feasibility. What looks good in a mockup doesn't always survive contact with a CMS.
Built and delivered the responsive site from start to finish.

Three roles, one person — end-to-end ownership

Research

Decision-makers need to scan, then trust.

Primary Insight
"They need to come back on their own terms, read it when it suits them, and find the contact when they’re ready."

Senior pharma and healthcare decision-makers, post-tradeshow. Busy people who’d already heard the pitch and weren’t looking to be sold to again.

PM + sales team interviews — credibility hinges on simplicity

Audience: senior pharma & healthcare decision-makers

Design Process

Two pages. Everything it needed. Nothing it didn’t.

The research made the structure obvious. A homepage that covered who Healthspire is, what they do, and how to get in touch — and a dedicated contact page. Anything more would have gotten in the way.

01
Everything on one page
The homepage covers About, Services, and Contact. Decision-makers shouldn’t have to hunt for basic information.
02
Designed for credibility
Visual credibility is a form of trust. If the site felt polished and considered, that said something about the company before anyone read a single word.
03
A clear path to contact
A dedicated contact page with a simple form — because the whole point of the site was to make it easy to start a conversation.

Wireframes

I reviewed every design before it got built.

Working in Figma alongside the designer, I flagged anything that wouldn’t translate cleanly to the platform before it became a problem in development. This saved significant rework time later.

Wireframes reviewed in Figma — feasibility flagged before development

The first round of designs leaned too hard on the CVS Health parent brand. Stakeholders wanted Healthspire to feel connected but distinct. We brought in the Healthspire teal and by the second round, it clicked.

Stakeholder review — unique brand identity within CVS guidelines

Final Solution

A professional digital
home, launched on time.

The site shipped before the next major conference. For the first time, the sales team had somewhere to send people. Prospective clients had a place to follow up on their own terms.

Final website — distinct brand, seamless UX

On time
Launch
Delivered before the next major tradeshow. The sales team had a professional digital destination ready when they needed it.
A real follow-up
Sales Team
The sales team could send prospects somewhere after a conversation. A credible destination that did part of the selling for them.
New channel
Lead generation
Healthspire went from having no digital presence to having a professional lead generation tool. Stakeholders saw it as a direct investment in future business.
Without a website, you don’t exist.

For B2B businesses in healthcare, a website isn’t a marketing asset. It’s proof of legitimacy. If someone can’t find you after a tradeshow conversation, that conversation didn’t happen. The most important thing I built wasn’t a homepage. It was a reason for prospects to trust the company enough to reach out.

Case Study · Print UX · Medicare

A letter that needed to work for people.

Medicare program members were missing their benefits because the letter meant to inform them was too hard to read — and buried the one thing they needed to act on. I redesigned the whole thing.

The Problem

The letter wasn't
working for its readers.

Members were calling support just to find a phone number that should have been impossible to miss. The type was too small for a 65+ audience, the layout gave nothing a sense of priority, and the one thing they needed to act on was buried in the fine print. It was a fixable problem — it just hadn’t been fixed yet.

Before vs after — document hierarchy redesigned

Research

I talked to the people
closest to the problem.

Account Representatives were fielding the same complaints every day. A few hours of interviews confirmed what they already knew — and gave me exactly what I needed to make the case for change.

Font size below accessibility standards
The type was 1–2pt too small for a 65+ audience, and the dense layout gave it no room to breathe. Reading it was exhausting.
Contact number impossible to find
It appeared once in body copy, then again in even smaller disclaimer text. Members couldn't locate it when they needed it most.

Qualitative interviews — 2 confirmed root causes

Design Response

Three changes that
solved both problems.

01
Move the contact info to the top
Replaced the decorative header image with a dedicated contact block. It's the first thing members see when they open the letter.
02
Create a scannable sidebar column
Key benefits surfaced in a shaded sidebar alongside the body text — readable at a glance without hunting through paragraphs.
03
Rebuild the typography from scratch
I increased the base size, established a real visual hierarchy, and gave the layout enough breathing room to feel approachable rather than dense.

Design process & key decisions

"

Program Support Stakeholder, on seeing the redesigned contact block for the first time

Iteration

One round of feedback,
thoughtfully addressed.

Stakeholders wanted imagery back in the design. I added thematic icons to the sidebar column — warmth that didn't compromise the clarity we'd worked hard to create.

One ask, one clean fix — approved unanimously

Final Solution

Approved across
the whole program.

The redesign was signed off unanimously — and rather than rolling out to the Medicare population alone, it was adopted across the entire program.

Final letter — clear, accessible, scannable

Clearer
Readability
Stakeholders called it a significant readability improvement — meaningful for an audience that depends on this letter to understand their coverage.
Found
Contact Info
Members could locate the contact number immediately. The calls about a buried phone number stopped.
All of it
Adopted Program-Wide
The design wasn't approved just for Medicare — it was rolled out across the entire program. Stakeholders called it a major success.
Good design is medium agnostic.

A letter and an app aren’t that different when you strip them back. Both need a clear hierarchy, an obvious next step, and type that doesn’t fight the reader. What changed on this project wasn’t the medium — it was whether someone could act on what they read.
